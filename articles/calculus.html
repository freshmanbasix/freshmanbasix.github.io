NUMBER & SET THEORY

MATHEMATICAL BUILDING BLOCKS

An Equation is a pair of expressions that have some stated relationship. They can be equal(=), strictly non-equal(≠) or unequal(<, ⋜, >, ⋝).

x + m = y [equation]

Inequalities or Intervals is a shorthand for equations where the expressions do not evaluate to the same. Intervals in particular describe a range within which an expression exists on the numberline

[inequality] x + m < y
[interval] x < m < y

Component names for the basic operations are listed below.

  • Addend + Addend = Sum
  • Minuend - Subtrahend = Difference
  • Term + Term = Sum (for use with both positive and negative terms)
  • Factor × Factor = Product
  • Dividend ÷ Divisor = Quotient
  • Numerator ÷ Denominator = Quotient

NUMBER PROPERTIES

commutative, associative etc.

The Commutative property describe whether the order of the operands matter in a given operation. Addition is commutative, since a + b = b + a for any {a, b ∈ ℝ}. Multiplication is another example, but subtraction is not since a - b ≠ b - a.

Which operands to resolve first under a given operation is determined by the Associative property. Addition and Multiplication are associative for example, because the following holds true: (a + b) + c = a + (b + c) and (a × b) × c = a × (b × c).

The Distributive property describe how a operand is distributed into an expression containing multiple terms under multiplication. a(b + c) = a×b + a×c.

If an operation is conducted with any two operands from the same set, and the result belongs to the same set, then this set is said to be Closed under this operation.

All operations have a Neutral Element, also refered to as the Identity. For any operand from some set a, there is a neutral(a) such that a + neutral(a) = a. In addition, the identity is 0, for multiplication, the identity is 1.

Inverse Elements are such that a operation will result in the identity for the given operation. As such, the inverse will be different depending on the number itself. For addition; a + inverse(a) = 0. For multiplication; b × inverse(b) = 1. Inverses in addition is the number with equal magnitude but opposite side of 0 on the numberline. In multiplication, the inverse is called the reciprocal, which is always 1 divided by the number itself, i.e inverse(b) = 1 ÷ b.

GEOMETRY

Geometry is the mathematical field concerned with space, dimensions, shapes, size and distances.

COORDINATE SYSTEMS

A coordinate system is a organized set of numerical values representing a point in a space of some dimensions. Three such system are the Cartesian, cylindrical and Spehrical coordinate systems. Different systems have differing benefits and drawbacks, mainly depending on what is being depicted, and what kind of operations are being performed.

CARTESIAN COORDINATES

Cartesian Coordinates is the common standard of representing space by letting each point in P = [x1, ..., xn] ∈ ℝn be represented by a multiple of 1 on n orthoganal axis. The regular xy-plane is often refered to as the cartesian plane. Euclidean Standard Space ℝ3, i.e physical 3D space, is usually represented as [x, y, z] in cartesian coordinates.

It is used as the basis as the common right-hand system, which is a method to standardize direction in a system. It is defined by taking your right hand and letting:

  • the thumbbe the positive x-axis
  • the index finger be the positive y-axis
  • the long finger be the positive z-axis
  • The circular rotation around the z-axis, from the positive x-axis to the negative, is considered to be of a positive magnitude

Positive rotation around the z axis represents position movement along the z-axis. A common example is the common threaded screw, which is screwed into a material by rotating it clockwise when the screw faces away from you.

POLAR COORDINATES

In a polar system, a ray between the point P is set as a polar axis, and the line through that pole has a single angle. The magnitude of this line from point to origin and the angle make up the polar coordinates. This system is usually best used for Cylindrical [r, θ, z] and Spherical [R, θ, ϕ] objects in space. This can be used to very easily calculate points of a spherical or cylindrical surface, or determine volumes etc.

COMPLEX NUMBERS ℂ

Complex Numbers are numbers with both real and imaginary components. The Imaginary Number Set 𝕀 is defined as all multiples of the Imaginary Unit i, 𝕀 = [∀(i × x)] such that x ∈ ℝ and i = sqrt{ -1 }. The square root of minus one, or any negative number, does not exist in the real number space, and as such lack a definition. The number 𝑖 was created to model the circumstances where real numbers are insufficient. Common applications are listed on the wikipedia page for the concept. Generally, any polynomial expression whose range does not contain y = 0 will require a complex solution, i.e any parabola completely above or below the x-axis.

Complex numbers are a special case of two dimensional vectors, existing as a point on the Imaginary Numberplane, which is made up of all linear combinations of the orthogonal Real and Imaginary Number lines. This space has the standard basis [1, i]. Commonly, complex numbers are denoted with the variable Z, with the boldface emphasising its vector properties. Each complex number Z will also have a Complex Conjugate, which exists on the mirror image, i.e opposite side, of the real line, commonly denoted with a bar.

There are several forms of the complex number, all offering different benefits when performing calculations

Z = α + 𝑖 × β (Cartesian Form)
Z |Z|(cos θ, 𝑖 × in θ) (Polar Form)
Z = |Z| × e 𝑖 × θ (Euler Form)

The different components of a complex number have some particular noation. The Imaginary component Im(Z) = β, The Real component Re(Z) = α. And lastly the Argument Arg(Z) = θ. The argument has a few special rules similar to that of the logarithm rules.

Arg(Z × W) = Arg(Z) + Arg(W)
Arg(Z ÷ W) = Arg(Z) - Arg(W)

TRIGONOMETRY

Trigonometry is the field pertaining to periodical functions.

TRIGONOMETRIC FUNCTIONS

The Trigonometric Functions describe real mathematical relationships between the hypotenuse and one of the sides of a right-angle triangle. By this definition, it maps the non-right angle in the triangle to a corresponding numerical value.

The primary functions are the sine, cosine and tangent functions.

They each have respective reciprocal functions secant, cosecant and cotangent.

The reverse concept of translating a real numerical value to an angle is achieved with the arc function.
