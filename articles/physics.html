SYSTEM OF UNITS

When measuring physical objects, each quantity needs a unit, both of which are designated by standard symbols to give a complete notational characteristic of what is being measured.

  • Quantities are properties that can exist as a multitude or magnitude. Examples are lenght, mass, time etc.
  • Units are the numerical amounts of things being measured. E.g metres, kilograms, seconds.
  • Each quantity and unit comes with a designation symbol which is an abbreviated shorthand for the quantity / unit itself. In the quantity case symbols generally take form of a stand-in variable which is a placeholder for the numerical value i calculations. Unit symbols are there to signify and represent the unit itself.
time (t) [s] seconds
<quantity name> (<quantity symbol>) [<unit symbol>] <unit name>

A international standard of units and notation has been established in the SI-unit system, which is the modern implementation of the metric system. A list of the base units can be found on the International System of Units Wikipedia page. The base units are a subset of core quantities, from which all other, derived units, are created.

When doing calculations, unit analysis is a technique where one removes the quantity symbols from an expression, leaving only the unit symbols. This is a way to quickly visualize how an expression can be simplified into another quantity or derived unit, deriving a proof that some operation gives the expected result.

NEWTONIAN MECHANICS

This section will cover the standard Newtonian laws of motion in the ocean.

CIRCULAR MOTIONS

Given any orbital motion, there is a period T [s] (i.e seconds per rotation), and a orbital radius r [m], which in turn give rise to a reciprocal frequency ƒ [Hz] = 1 / T, i.e rotations per second.

The angular velocity v [m/s] is the instantaneous movement in metres per second in a direction tangential to the circumference of the circle.

v = frac{ 2π }{ T } × r = 2πƒr

A closely related quantity is the angular frequency ω = 2πƒ (radians per second).
