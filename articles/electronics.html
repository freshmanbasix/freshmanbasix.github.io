AC CIRCUITS

Alternating Current (AC) is a current induced by a periodically oscillating voltage. It takes the form of a sinusoid waveform, with alternating positive and negative peaks of amplitude. The induced current is a function of the alternating voltage and time. The two will follow each other with some phaseshift determined by resistance and reactance, see this practical paper on phase shift. AC allows for information transmission by encoding data in the sinusiod pattern.

Resistance is the purely real component of impedance, R = Re(Z), which exist due to resistors. A resistor has an impedance ZR = R. Resistance is a strictly linear property achieved by passing current through materials that have poor conductive qualities, e.g ceramics, glas etc. Resistance will behave the same in both AC and DC circuits.

SAFETY

100-200mA of current will kill an adult human being. Do not fuck around with electricity. When performing repairs or work on circuits connected to the mains, see some security 101 tips below.

  • Unplug the device AND shut it off completely before tinkering with its inside.
  • Put a layer of proper insulation between yourself and the floor.
  • Don't tinker while tired.
  • Don't work with wired headphones connected to the device that is being repaired.
  • Use insulated tools.

SECURITY MECHANISMS

Below are descriptions of some of the most important security systems in place in modern electrical devices, how they work and what protections they don't offer. Additionally, this Youtube video on safety for radio amateurs might offer some further information.

FUSES

Fuses are current sensing elements located at the input of the live wire. They are designed to open the circuit if a current threshhold is crossed. A fuse will protect from shorts between live and neutral.

RESIDUAL CURRENT DETECTION

An RCD, "Jordfelsbrytare" in Swedish, is a current sensor circuit connected over the live and neutral wire at the mains level, essentially at the wall socket. It responds if the current running through the live wire is different from the one running through the netural wire.

This system protects from ground leakage. It does NOT protect devices from short circuits between live and neutral.

PROTECTIVE GROUND

Protective Ground/Earth (PE/PG) is used to divert energy into a safe direction in the case of a faulty wire that might connect a chassis to the live wire. It limits the risk of serious injury and electrocution. By modern safety standards, all outlets must be equipped with protective ground pins.

One important note is that it only connects the chassis itself to ground. When doing repairs, faults in the internal circuitry may still pose a risk of unexpected electrocution unless personal protection is used, i.e insulated floor cover

DOUBLE INSULATION

Primarily an alternative to protective ground. By doubling down on the insulation, a circuit can be protected from any ground leakage. Any parts that might electrify the chassis or other parts of the device by mistake are thoroughly insulated, decreasing the danger considerably. This measure is most commonly applied to deviced used outdoors.

BATTERIES

While commonly operating at low voltages, e.g 12VDC, low inner resistance can induce very large currents (1000+ Amps) on a short circuit. On top of electrocution and fire, batteries contain chemicals that may cause additional harm. Some useful safety precautions are:

  • Be sure to use properly insulated tools when working on battery powered equipment and devices.
  • Never wear rings or metal objects on your hands.
