Its constituent parts are explained in detail, but it is introduced here. As seen in the figure below, the model identifies a Transmitted Message MSGin. A Modulation and Demodulation step. Inbetween them is the Channel, i.e space through which the Signal S travels. Within the channel, the signal will be affected by Noise, which attenuates it, giving rise to a Path Loss Lp.

ISOTROPIC RADIATED POWER

The Isotropic Radiation Case is a common reference case that sets up a fundamental starting point from which real examples are derived. It imagines the Transmitted Power PTX [W] radiating from a single point monopole antenna in a perfect sphere with area A = 4πd 2.

Along the surface area of the sphere at distance d, the transmitted power is uniformly distributed. This means that the amount of power per metre squared along the spheres surface is constant. This gives rise to the Irradiance I[W / m2], sometimes refered to as Power Flux Density or Intensity. More information can be found at the wikipedia article on the subject

I = frac{ PTX }{ A } = frac{ |E|2 }{ Zfs }
Zfs ≈ 377Ω is the free space impedance. Note also that intensity is sometimes abreviated as S, so be careful not to confuse it with the symbol for signal.

The Antenna Aperture Ae [m2] is the area projection of the receiving antenna on the plane normal to the incident signal wave. In the isotropic case, the aperture is a simplified Ae,iso = λ2 / 4π. The relationship between the aperture and the physical size will be slightly missaligned. If Ae = Aphys, that would imply that the aperture efficiency is 100% which cannot be achieved in reality. In practice, the actual aperture is determined by measuring the ratio between received power to what should theoretically be received.

Ae = frac{ PRX }{ I }

The power that actually arrives at the receiving antenna is denoted the Received Power PRX[W]. This will ALWAYS be a fraction of the transmitted power, directly proportional to aperture and area. PTX = PRX is impossible to achieve in reality. Received power is measured in watts, meaning that given some intensity (watts per metres squared) at the point of the antenna, times the aperture (metres squared) will equal the power. We also refer to this received power as the Signal S.

PRX = Ae × I = PTX × frac{ Ae }{ A }

The difference between transmitted and received power is what makes up the Path Loss Lp. Loss has no unit and is given in decibels. It originates from any and all things that exist and interfere along the path between transmitter and receiver.

PRX,iso = PTX,iso × frac{ Ae,iso }{ A } = PTX,iso × frac{ 1 }{ Lfs }
Lpath = frac{PTX }{PRX }
Here, Lfs = (4πdλ)2 is the free space path loss.

GAIN

Antenna design can achieve variations of antenna amplification factors. For example, a directional antenna will redistribute the even density into particular regions. As such, the Antenna Gain G is a multiplier which can exist on both transmitting and receiving end. One thing to note is that real antenna aperture is proportional to the gain and isotropic aperture:

Ae = GRX × Ae,iso

The received power is a result of transmitted power and gains:

PRX = PTX × GTX × GRX × Ae,iso
(PRX)dBW = (PTX)dBW + (GTX)dBi + (GRX)dBi + (Ae,iso)dB

EFFECTIVE ISOTROPIC RADIATED POWER

A important value is the EIRP, which is a conversion from a real transmitted power to the level it would be if the transmitter actually was isotropic. It is important to know as local regulations often limit the transmission effects in EIRP.

PEIRP = PTX × GTX

NOISE

Noise is simply attenuating factors decreasing the quality of the signal. Some general sources are:

  • Interference from other transmitting antennas in the same range.
  • Man made noise, generated by electrical devices. Mostly only relevant at lower frequency bands.
  • Galactic noise, coming from space. Mainly relevant in higher frequency ranges.

THERMAL NOISE

Thermal Noise is the most important noise factor which originates from Brownian motion in the components. It is dependent on temperature and is proportional to Boltzmans constant k. For a given frequency, the Noise Figure F is a factor representing how much larger the Total Noise Level N is compared to the thermal noise of the receiver.

N = F × k × T0 × B

kT0 is the spectral density [W / Hz] and the temperature needs to be given i Kelvins. k = 1.38 × 10-23

SIGNAL TO NOISE RATIO

The Signal to Noise Ratio, denoted SNR or S/N is the relationship between Signal strenght, i.e received power, to noise.

Signal = PRX = frac{ PTX }{ Path Loss L}

SERVICE REQUIREMENTS

Depending on the kind of information we wish to transmit, a given bandwidth will require a certain SNR in order to be successfully interpreted. This value is generally read from a table.

LINK BUDGET

With given values for the variables described above, a value can be determined for the SNR, allowing us to determine whether it is within the range necessary for the circumstance. The actual value of the Signal S, not to be confused with intensity, is the received power:

frac{ Signal }{ Noise } = frac{ PTX }{ Path Loss × Noise }
(S / N)dB = (PTX)dBW - (Loss)dB - (F)dB - (k × T0)dB - (Bandwidth)dB
Converting values to a logarithmic scale makes calculation much easier. Just keep in mind which reference point is used, i.e 1mW (dBm), 1W (dBW), isotropic (dBi) etc.
(PTX)dBm = 10 × Log10 ( PTX / 0.001 )
(PTX)dBW = 10 × Log10 ( PTX, W / 1 )
(PTX)dBi = 10 × Log10 ( Sreal / Siso )

MODULATION

This section contain summaries and background on the methods of Modulating, i.e embedding discernable data into, a signal. For any electromagnetic wave, there are only three mutable components that can be changed to achieve a pattern. First is the waves magnitude, or peak power, denoted as the Amplitude A. The Linear Frequency ƒ determines how many times per second the wave oscillates. Lastly, the Phase Shift ϕ represent the offset. The frequency is also the defining factor in the Angular Frequency ω = 2πƒ. This represent how many radians per second the wave changes. In the sinusoidal case, given these four components, any wave s can be expressed as a function of time:

s(t) = A × sin( ωt + ϕ )

ANALOG MODULATION

Analog modulation uses continous waveforms as a Modulating Signal, making it a appropriate mdoulation scheme for voice, music etc. In short, the process of modulating a signal makes use of a Carrier Wave Signal, which is the baseline that receivers align against, and then applying a Modulating Wave Signal, which is the container for the data, achieving a Modulated Wave Signal.

AMPLITUDE MODULATION

Amplitude Modulation AM is a linear method of encoding a carrier wave with a modulating wave that varies in amplitude. The result is a modulated wave with the frequency of the carrier wave whose envelope is identical to the modulating wave. The amplitude variations carry the data.

Quality of an AM signal can be expressed as the AM index m which is the ratio of the sideband ampllitude over the carrier amplitude. This ratio can also be expressed in percent, usually then refered to as AM depth. Rohde Schwarz have an excellent Youtube video on the subject.

m = frac{sideband amplitude}{carrier amplitude}
NdB = 20 × log10 (m/2)

The bandwidth of an AM signal is b = 2 × fm.

FREQUENCY MODULATION FM/PM

A non-linear method of alternating the frequency instead of amplitude as the encoding. As the modulating signal varies, so will the carrier. As such, we have on top of the carrier signal frequency fc, the Nominal Center Frequency f0, which is the carrier without any modulating signal applied to it. The Maximum Deviation Δfmax is the maximum width of frequency range that the modulated signal varies from the nominal center. Meanwhile, the modulating frequency fm is, like in the AM case, the variation range of the modulating signal itself.

The relationship between deviation and modulating frequency is described by the Deviation Ratio β, sometimes abbreviated DR. Different types of transmissions are divided into either Wideband FM, for beta ⋝ 1, and Narrowband FM, β < 1. Using Carson's Rule, the bandwidth can be approximated as double the sum of the max deviation and the modulating frquency width.

deviation ratio β = frac{ Δfmax }{ fm }
bandwidth ≈ 2(fm + Δfmax) = 2 × fm × (1 + β)

DIGITAL MODULATION

Discrete modulation applies a discrete combination of patterns to express a finite number of symbols. It makes use of a Symbol Set, which is the alphabet of different symbols that can be transmitted. A Symbol Duration T[s], which is the reciprocal of the Symbol Rate R[s-1]. There are multitudes of different types of digital modulation schemes. One useful resource for identifying them is the ITU classification codes for different radio emission types.

One very basic example is the On-Off Keying (OOK), which simply toggles the transmitter on and of, encoding the keys to the symbols into the pattern with which the transmission is toggled. An example could be morse code. Generally speaking, OOK is most appropriate for binary symbols, but can be used to express longer more complex symbol sets.

One important note is that higher datarate leads to lower SNR in the digital case. The signal in this case is represented by the Error Bitrate Eb.

SNRdigital = Eb / N0 = (PTX × T) / N0 = PTX / (R × N0)

FREQUENCY SHIFT KEYING

Encode symbols by switching between a discrete number of different frequencies, also refered to as tones. The number of frequencies are referenced in the shorthand M-ary FSK, where M is equal to the number of tones and the bitdepth of each symbol is log2(M). Binary FSK is a common term for 2-FSK.

Tones generated by the same oscillator are called Coherent FSK and allow for closer spacing of the tones. If multiple oscillators generate the different tones, the implementation is Non-Coherent FSK, which is cheaper to produce, but risks Inter Symbol Interference(ISI) if tones are packed close.

The different tones are deviations from the nominal center, and that distance is called the Deviation fd, sometimes Δfc or shift. The Bandwidth BW is a function of both deiviation and Bitrate fb. The most appropriate bandwidth is usually chosen by finding the Modulation Index h. The optimal symbol detection is achieved when h ⋝ 1.

Some FSK methods can operate with a modulation index of less than 1, e.g Minimum Shift Keying.
Bandwidth BW = 2(fd + fb)
mod index h = frac{ 2 × fd }{ fb }

PHASE SHIFT KEYING

Differences in phase of the carrier are used to indicate symbols. The symbol scheme is illustrated in a constellation diagram. Each symbol si in the set has some amplitude and phase, represented by distance from origin and angle around the z-axis. A received symbol r has levels of error probabilites ni, represented by the respective distances to each symbol. The value of r is resolved by choosing the symbol nearest to r.

Frequency and amplitude are generally kept constant in phase shift keying.

APERTURE ANTENNAS

Usually known as Parabolic Reflectors, that concentrate the incoming wave onto the actual antenna. The parabolic dish needs to be approximately 5 times larger than the wavelength. With a diameter D > 5 × λ. The aperture is approximately Ae ≈ 0.5 × frac{ πD2 }{ 4 } ≈ 0.4D2. What we know about the aperture being proportional to the gain:

POLARIZATION

Polarization describe the orientation of the oscillations in a transverse wave. This means the orientation of the antenna matters and if the receiving antenna has a different polarization than the incoming wave, some or all of the signal will be lost. In the case of line-of-sight transmission, the antennas must have the same polarization. The Ionosphere rotates the polarization of a wave, meaning that bouncing a signal of it, or transmitting through it needs to take that into account.

CIRCULAR POLARIZATION

Designing two orthogonal antennas fed by a quarter wave delay will achieve a polarization that rotates.

ELECTROMAGNETIC FIELD PROPERTIES

The Electronic Field E or Magnetic Field H might be dominant depending on the type of antenna. Dipoles will usually be E-dominant, while small loop antennas will be H-dominant. Electric field strength is measured in volts per meter (V/m), and magnetic field strenght is measured in amperes per meter (A/m).

NEAR & FAR FIELD

When measuring antenna properties, there are two categories of fields:

  • The Near Field is the area around the antenna that falls within a small fraction of the antennas wavelength. In this area, it is very difficult to determine field strengths.
  • The Far Field is the distance on a order of wavelength AWAY from the antenna. This area is more relevant for RF communication and it is easier to determine field strenghts here.

FIELD STRENGHT

The strenght of EM fields from an antenna depend on a set of factors. The first of which is distance d. Field strenght is inversely proportional to the distance. This means is will change rapidly depending on the distance from the antenna.

Less impactful, but still important is the Power P. Field strength increase with the square root of the power, meaning the cranking the power up is not especially optimal to achieve a increased effective output. The effective transmitted power has several components listed below. Combining them all give a value for the average transmitted power Pavg = PTX × M × I × k

  • The peak power PTX, i.e the concrete peak power output of the transmitting antenna. Usually listed in dB.
  • The Modulation (Duty) Factor M, which is a factor representing the effect of the modulation type on the output power. This value is generally an estimated constant aquired from a reference table.
  • The Intermittency Factor I, which is the averaged proportion of the time over 6 minutes that is spent transmitting, i.e talking vs listening.
  • The attenuation i the feeding cable, i.e cable loss k is a factor between 1 and 0 that represent how much transmitted power actually reach the antenna.

Antenna Gain G is a factor multiplying the effect of the field. It is commonly given in dB, and represent the worst case in exposure calculations.

The value of the Electric Field strength E is a function of distance, power and gain.

E = frac{ sqrt{ 30 × P × G } }{ d }
Note that all units must be in linear space for this formula to work.

Provided the electric or magnetic field os known, this can be used to determine the Radiation Intensity S (W/m2 by applying the intrinsic impedance of free space Z0 ≈ 120π ≈ 377Ω

|S| = frac{ |E|2 }{ Z0 } = Z0 × |H|2

TRANSMISSION LINES

The Transmission line, a.k.a Feeder line is the physical medium through which the energy is transported between trnasmitter and antenna. This can be a cable, a more rigid element, or even a intrinsic part of the antenna itself.

CABLES

NON-COAX

The ladder line is a rigid construction that separates the positive and negative lead of the transmission lite with a constant physical distance. This makes it immune to noise as the magnetic field effects cancel each other out. It commonly has a much higher characteristic impedance, making it unpractical in some situation

VELOCITY FACTOR

The speed of light inside depends on the medium it travels through. For any physical cable. The Veolcity Factor v is the ratio of the speed through a cable to the ideal speed in a vacuum. The real speed through the cable is thereby defined as ccable = v × cvacuum. The physical length that properly represent one single wavelenght is defined as λcable = v × λ.

MATCHING

Impedance Matching a.k.a Antenna Tuning is the process of matching up the impedances between the transmitter and the load, i.e antenna. Impedances are complex values where we want to separate two factors; the first being source impedance Z0 = R0 + j0, which is the impedance in the transmitter. This impedance is, most of the time, purely real, i.e no reactance component. The second factor is the load impedance Zl = (Rl + Rrad) + jXl , make sure to not confuse the load impedance with the inductive reactance.

Note that antenna reactance is highly dependent on frequency and we are only "tuning" it for a particular sub-range close to ƒ = frac{1}{ sqrt{ 4π2LC} }.
  • R0 is the internal resistance of the transmitter.
  • Rl is the resistive loss of the antenna. Energy dissipated over this resistance element will be lost as heat in the antenna.
  • Rrad is the antenna radiation resistance which is the desired effective element. The energy dissipated here is useful electromagnetic radiation beamed into space.
  • Xl is the complex component, reactance, of the antennas impedance

Matching is achieved when the impedances are the same in transmitter, transmission line and antenna. Rtransmitter = Rfeeder cable = Rantenna and Xantenna = |XL| - |XC| = 0 will fullfill this criteria. This relationship will give a VSWR of 1, which is the optimal value. Texas Instruments have compiled a very useful pdf on tuning HF Antennas which describe the matching process in further detail.

Connecting a very poorly matched antenna can damage the transmitter.

SIGNAL REFLECTION

The relationship between power transmitted through the transmission line, forward power, and the possible reflected power is important to achieving matching. When impedances are not even, power is reflected back instead of radiated out into space, effectively being wasted.

RETURN LOSS

The Return Loss describes the arithmetic difference between transmitted and reflected power to an antenna in dBm, P forward - P reflected = return loss. Larger values for the return loss ⇒ less power wasted.

REFLECTION COEFFICIENT Γ

The reflection coefficient Γ is the ratio between the difference in impedances to the total impedance. This is the value we want to be 0. One can also determine Γ by ratio of the reflected and forward amplitudes Vr:Vf

Γ = frac{ Zload - Zsource }{ Zload + Zsource } = frac{ Vr }{ Vf }

VOLTAGE STANDING WAVE RATIO

The Voltage Standing Wave Ratio (VSWR) is the ratio of the peak and bottom of the standing wave that appear when a transmitted forward voltage wave vf(t), Vf sin(ωt + ϕ1), is superimposed by a reflected voltage wave vr(t), Vr sin(ωt + ϕ2).

The standing wave is the resulting sum of the forward and reflected voltages, with a maximum VPEAK, and bottom VMIN.

VSWR = frac{ VPEAK }{ VMIN } = frac{ 1 + |Γ| }{ 1 - |Γ| }

SMITH CHARTS

A Smith Chart is a graphical method to illustrate the relative difference between source and load impedance. It uses a normalized scale and bends the vertical axis of a cartesian plane into a circle that folds over itself with the planar origin in the centre of the circle with R = 1.

  • The horizontal axis represents the resistive component. Each vertical gridline circle represents a resistive ratio value.
  • The vertical axis represents the reactance component. The top half inductive, bottom half capacitive. Each vertical gridline curve flowing from the right out towards the circumference represent a ractance ratio value.

  • Each point on the chart takes the relative complex value, frac{ Zl }{ Z0 }, i.e

    ( x, y ) = frac{ Rl + jXl }{ R0 + jX0 }

RULES & SAFETY

Transmitting RF waves is a legislated activity in all countries. This is mainly due to the need for controlling for interference and safety. These two aspects will be covered in this section.

LAWS & REGULATIONS

Radio amateurs are partly exempt from the regulations regarding constructing electrical devices and transmitting devices. You as the designer are solely responsible for your devices being safe to operate, and not a cause of interference. You may NOT build and sell mains-operated home-built equipment on the market on a commercial basis.

Furthermore, the Radio Amateur may NOT:

  • Build transmitters for use outside the amateur frequency bands.
  • Modify an amateur radio transmitter for use outside the amateur radio frequency bands.
  • Alter a CE labeled transmitter to work outside the amateur radio frequency bands.
  • Restore a CE labeled transmitter to original function, after it has been modified for use within the amateur radio frequency bands.

Laws regulating the requirements on security when operating powered devices are the Elsäkerhetslag (2016:732), and Elsäkerhetsförordning (2017:218). The laws regulating use of radio transmitters on are the Radioutrustningslag (2016:392), and the Radioutrustningsförordning (2016:394).

EMF & ANTENNA SAFETY

Some consideration need to be made when operating long-range or outdoors antennas. Some general safety guidelines are summarized below.

  • Weather, even just a fog or light rain, can potentially build up significant static charges which create a risk for electrocution and falling (due to the antenna usually being elevated).
  • A antenna MAY NOT be connected to an existing lightning rod for a building, but must rather be protected separately against lightning.
  • The best protection for connected devices is to sever contact with the antenna during bad weather.

Radio antennas emit non-ionizing radiation, with established exposure limits to protect the public. Radiation at the radio band frequencies can potentially heat up materials and cause harmful effects. The unit used to measure harmful exposure is the SAR(Specific Absorption Rate)[W/kg]. It is generally difficult to effectively measure in the field and corresponding electrical or magnetic field strengths have been tabulated. The formal exposure limits are set at 2% of the level that has been shown to cause harm. It also represents the average power over 6 minute intervals due to heating from radiation is a slow process.

Important regulations are the EU Council Recommendation 1999/519/EC. In Sweden, the Strålsäkerhetsmyndighetens Allmänna Råd SSMFS 2008:18 give a good overview of the relevant levels. Furthermore, Arbetsmiljöverket in Sweden have issued recommendations and information on their website regardingelectromagnetic fields in the workplace.

Some quick useful pointers on EMF safety are:

  • In the frequency range of 10-400 MHz the exposure limits are extra low due to a resonance phenomenon associated with the size of the human body.
  • Balcony and indoor antennas can give rise to unexpectedly high field strengths where people live.
  • Directional antennas can generate very high field strengths in the main direction of the antenna.
  • Feedlines for non-symmetric antennas can radiate considerably and cause high field strengths at the transmission site.
  • Power amplifiers and antenna matching units without proper shielding rise to strong fields at the transmission site.
